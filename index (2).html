HEMOGLOBIN
α₂β₂ tetramer · oxygen transport protein
A quaternary protein with 4 subunits and 4 iron-containing heme groups. The workhorse of your blood — every breath you take depends on it.
WHY IT MATTERS
Hemoglobin carries oxygen from your lungs to every cell in your body — 270 million molecules per red blood cell, making ~2 billion trips in a cell's 120-day lifetime. Without it, tissues would suffocate within minutes. A single amino acid substitution (Val→Glu at position 6 of the β-chain) causes sickle cell anemia, one of the most common genetic diseases worldwide, affecting 300,000 newborns per year.
574 amino acids 4 subunits 64,500 Da Quaternary structure
⚠️ Sickle Cell Anemia
LEVEL 02
🛡️
IMMUNOGLOBULIN G (IgG)
Y-shaped antibody · immune defense protein
The most abundant antibody in human blood. A Y-shaped protein that recognizes and neutralizes pathogens with extreme specificity.
WHY IT MATTERS
IgG antibodies are your immune system's precision weapons — each one binds exactly one molecular target (antigen). They neutralize viruses, mark bacteria for destruction, and cross the placenta to protect newborns. Over 100 FDA-approved antibody drugs (monoclonal antibodies) treat cancer, autoimmune diseases, and COVID-19. Understanding IgG structure is fundamental to modern medicine — disulfide bonds hold the Y-shape together, and breaking them destroys function.
1,320 amino acids 4 chains (2H + 2L) 150,000 Da Quaternary + disulfide bonds
⚠️ Autoimmune Disease
LEVEL 03
⚡
INSULIN
Pancreatic hormone · blood glucose regulator
A small but critical hormone that controls blood sugar in every person on Earth — and whose absence or malfunction underlies diabetes.
WHY IT MATTERS
Insulin tells cells to absorb glucose from the blood. Without it, glucose accumulates, cells starve, and organs fail — this is diabetes. Over 537 million people worldwide live with diabetes. Insulin was the first protein to have its amino acid sequence determined (Sanger, 1955 Nobel Prize) and the first genetically engineered protein drug (1982). Three disulfide bonds lock its structure — alter one and it loses its ability to bind the insulin receptor.
51 amino acids 2 chains (A+B) 5,808 Da 3 disulfide bonds
⚠️ Type 1 & 2 Diabetes
LEVEL 04
🧱
COLLAGEN
Triple helix · most abundant protein in the body
The structural scaffold of skin, bone, tendons, and blood vessels. Makes up 30% of total body protein — more than any other protein.
WHY IT MATTERS
Collagen is the body's building material — it holds everything together. Its rope-like triple helix is one of the strongest biological structures known. But it has a critical weakness: every third amino acid must be Glycine (the only AA small enough for the interior). Vitamin C is required to stabilize the helix — without it, collagen falls apart causing scurvy (a disease that killed sailors for centuries). Over 1,000 mutations in collagen genes cause brittle bone disease, skin disorders, and blood vessel rupture.
~1,400 AA per chain 3 intertwined chains ~300,000 Da Gly-X-Y repeat structure
⚠️ Scurvy · Ehlers-Danlos · OI
LEVEL 05
☠️
PRION PROTEIN (PrP)
PrPᶜ (normal) → PrPˢᶜ (misfolded)
The most mysterious protein in biology — the same sequence, two different shapes, one of which is infectious. No DNA, no RNA, no virus. Just a misfolded protein.
WHY IT MATTERS
Prions shattered the central dogma. Before 1982, scientists believed only DNA and RNA could carry infectious information. Stanley Prusiner proved that a misfolded protein alone could be an infectious agent — and won the 1997 Nobel Prize for it. Misfolded PrP converts normal PrP into the misfolded form just by contact, creating a chain reaction that destroys the brain. Prion diseases (CJD, BSE "mad cow," kuru) are 100% fatal. Understanding prions also taught us about Alzheimer's and Parkinson's, where similar protein aggregation occurs.
253 amino acids 1 chain ~35,000 Da Conformational disease
☠️ CJD · BSE · Alzheimer's connection
